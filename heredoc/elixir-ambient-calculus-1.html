Intro

This is the first post of an on-going series.

Ambient Calculus

Briefly, the ambient calculus is a mathematical abstraction and a member of a more general family of "process calculi". Surprisingly simple yet Turing-complete phenomenon are a dime a dozen these days1, but even considering the vast zoo of automata and calculi known to computer scientsts, the Ambient Calculus has some properties I find particularly interesting.

Here's the original paper if you've got time. Alternatively, here's the summary from wikipedia:

The ambient calculus is a process calculus devised by Luca Cardelli and Andrew D. Gordon in 1998, and used to describe and theorise about concurrent systems that include mobility. Here mobility means both computation carried out on mobile devices (i.e. networks that have a dynamic topology), and mobile computation (i.e. executable code that is able to move around the network). The ambient calculus provides a unified framework for modeling both kinds of mobility. It is used to model interactions in such concurrent systems as the Internet.

Elixir

All this talk of concurrent systems and mobile code at the core of the Ambient Calculus immediately got me thinking about an implementation in Elixir, just for fun. It's an intriguing prospect because Elixir, running on top of the Erlang virtual machine, already implicitly supports hot-swapping code, distributed computing, and has lots of sophisticated primitives for expressing ideas about concurrency. I'll cover very briefly the various concurrency primitives that will be most useful in Ambient Calculus implementation later.

Concurrency in Elixir

  • Nodes are simply an Erlang (or in our case, Elixir) runtime capable of communicating with other such runtimes. From the Erlang point of view, there is some documentation here and from the Elixir point of view you can read about it here. With Elixir you can bootstrap a node like this iex --sname node_name --cookie group_name -S mix. To get a list of known nodes: Node.list(). To get the name of the current Node: Node.self(). Nodes don't automatically connect so you need Node.connect().. we'll talk later about auto-joining clusters later.

  • Agents, as state-wrappers, are a lot like objects except that they are concurrency-friendly by default.

  • Tasks are processes, often short-lived and created dynamically.

  • Supervisors are processes which supervise other processes. Supervisors may be nested.

Project Scope

The goal is to make a library/system wherein one can actually experiment with building things made of Ambient primitives. Such a system will necessarily have a working knowledge of AC reduction semantics, but it's worth noting that purely symbolic manipulation of the calculus itself is not really in scope here.

The point of the implementation discussed here is to use Ambients, and it's not supposed to be extremely rigorous2 or pure.3

Definitions

To establish a working definition of an Ambient for the purposes of the implementation, here are few things about ambients that are important.

  1. Ambients are kinds of mobile container with unique names.
  2. Ambients may hold key/values; that's called the ambient's namespace
  3. Ambients may hold programs; that's called the ambient'sambient's progspace
  4. Ambients may be hold other ambients; that's called the ambient's children.

Operations

Most of the core operations on ambients are implied by the simple definition above.

  • Ambients may be copied. This operation derives a new ambient from an existing one with an identical namespace / progspace. The new ambient becomes a sibling with the old one, under the same parent ambient.
  • Ambients can be entered and exited.
  • Ambients may be opened, i.e. their membrane is dissolved and what was once their namespace/progspace then belongs to the parent ambient.

Relationships

  • When ambient B is inside ambient A, then A is said to be the parent of B
